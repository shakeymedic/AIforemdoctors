AI Tools for Emergency Medicine Doctors:
A Practical Guide

Dr Jake Turner · ST6 Emergency Medicine, Birmingham Heartlands Hospital · Written with AI assistance · 2025–2026

A practical guide to the six AI tools that are most useful for EM doctors in their day-to-day professional work — focused on portfolio, research, quality improvement, teaching, and organisation, rather than direct clinical decision-making.

Perplexity Claude Gemini NotebookLM ChatGPT Microsoft Copilot
⚠ The one rule you must never break: Never enter patient-identifiable information into any of these tools — no names, NHS numbers, dates of birth, or anything from clinical records. All use cases in this guide concern non-clinical professional work only. The clinician remains responsible for all clinical decisions; AI is a tool, not a substitute for professional judgement.

Portfolio & Reflective Practice

Stop staring at a blank reflective log

AI won't write your portfolio for you — but it provides the scaffolding to write it yourself, faster and with better structure. RCEM's September 2025 position statement explicitly approves this approach.

Turning rough notes into a structured reflection

The hardest part of a reflective log is starting. Most trainees know what they want to say but can't shape it. The workflow is straightforward: jot bullet-point notes immediately after a significant case or experience — even a few sentences on your phone. Then use Claude to transform those raw notes into a Gibbs or Driscoll reflection. Your voice, your experience, your insight — just properly structured.

Try this Claude
Here are my rough notes from a resuscitation I led last week: [paste your bullet points]

Structure these into a reflective log using Gibbs' Reflective Cycle. Keep my voice and do not add any clinical details not in my notes. Where my reflection is thin, suggest specific questions I could consider to deepen it — don't answer them for me.

What RCEM says is and isn't acceptable

RCEM's September 2025 position statement is clear. Acceptable: using AI to structure reflections, improve clarity, suggest reflective prompts, and explore different perspectives. Not acceptable: fabricating experiences, submitting AI content without genuine personal input, or using AI to avoid reflecting at all. The experience and critical thinking must be genuinely yours.

ARCP gap analysis

Before an ARCP, paste a summary of your portfolio entries into Claude and ask it to map them against the RCEM curriculum. Ask it to identify thin areas and suggest what types of entries would fill gaps. This turns a daunting exercise into a clear action plan.

Try this Claude
Here is a summary of my portfolio entries from the past year: [paste summary]

I'm an ST4 EM trainee. Map my evidence against the RCEM curriculum competencies. Tell me which areas look well-evidenced, which look thin, and suggest what types of portfolio entries would fill the gaps before my ARCP.

Personal statements and applications

Give Claude your raw notes about your achievements and what the post requires — then ask it to identify your strongest narrative thread before drafting anything. This is more useful than asking it to write a statement from scratch, as it forces clarity about what makes your application distinctive.

Which tool to use

  • →Claude — best for writing and long documents. Its large context window can hold your entire portfolio summary.
  • →Copilot — if your portfolio is in Word, Copilot can work directly within the document.
  • →Gemini — ideal if your portfolio is in Google Docs; works in-context without copy-pasting.

The RCEM 2025 position — key phrase

"AI may be used to structure reflections and enhance clarity, but the experience, insight, and critical thinking must be genuinely the trainee's own."

Research & Evidence

Find the evidence. Understand the paper. In half the time.

AI has transformed literature searching. Know which tool to use — and remember that all of them can still misrepresent sources. Always verify cited references yourself before using them.

Start with Perplexity for any literature question

Perplexity is the first port of call for any evidence question. It searches the web in real time and cites every claim, so you can click through to the source paper directly. Think of it as a research assistant who has already read everything and synthesises it with references attached. Its Deep Research mode conducts multi-step autonomous research, producing comprehensive reports in under four minutes.

Try this Perplexity
What does the current evidence say about point-of-care ultrasound for diagnosing pulmonary oedema in the emergency department? Summarise the key RCTs and systematic reviews from the last five years, including sample sizes, key findings, and limitations. Focus on UK-applicable evidence.

Go deeper with Claude for full document analysis

Once Perplexity has given you an overview, use Claude for the detailed work. You can upload entire systematic reviews, NICE guidelines, and RCEM best practice documents and interrogate them all in a single conversation — particularly useful for guideline comparison and critical appraisal preparation.

Try this Claude
I've uploaded this systematic review. Help me critically appraise it using the CASP framework. Identify the main strengths and weaknesses, assess the risk of bias across included studies, and tell me how applicable the findings are to a UK district general ED. Be direct about where the evidence is weak.

Journal club prep: the three-tool workflow

Upload the paper to NotebookLM and generate an Audio Overview to understand the broad picture on your commute. Upload it to Claude for a structured CASP appraisal. Then use Perplexity to find papers that support or challenge the findings. What used to take a Sunday afternoon now takes about 20 minutes.

Verify every reference

ChatGPT and Claude can fabricate paper references with plausible-sounding authors, journals, and DOIs. This is known as hallucination. Always verify any cited reference yourself in PubMed or Google Scholar before including it in anything you write or present. Perplexity is safer because it links to real sources, but it can still misrepresent what those sources actually say.

Research workflow

  • 1Perplexity — first stop for any evidence question. Always cites sources.
  • 2Claude — upload full papers and guidelines for in-depth appraisal and comparison.
  • 3NotebookLM — convert papers into audio for commute listening.

  • →Research Rabbit — free tool for tracing citation trails visually

QIP & Data Analysis

Your audit data analysed — without a statistician

ChatGPT's Code Interpreter is genuinely transformative for doctors with audit data and no coding background. Upload a spreadsheet, describe what you want, and it writes and runs the analysis for you.

Analysing your data

ChatGPT Plus includes Code Interpreter (Advanced Data Analysis). Upload a CSV or Excel file, describe what you want in plain English, and it writes the Python code, runs it, and returns charts and results. No coding required.

Try this ChatGPT
I've uploaded a CSV of door-to-antibiotic times for sepsis patients over three months. Columns: date, time_minutes, shift (day/evening/night), day_of_week.

1. Calculate median and IQR for door-to-antibiotic time
2. Show what percentage met the 60-minute target
3. Break down by shift and day of week to identify patterns
4. Create a histogram of the distribution

SPC charts for RCEM QIPs

SPC charts are required for RCEM portfolio QIP submissions. ChatGPT generates them from your data — including mean, UCL, LCL, and automatic flagging of rule violations.

Try this ChatGPT
Using the uploaded data, create an I-MR SPC chart. Calculate mean, UCL, and LCL. Highlight any points violating standard SPC rules: a point beyond 3 sigma, 8+ consecutive points one side of the mean, or 6 consecutive points in a trend. Label each violation clearly on the chart and explain what it means.

The full QIP workflow

1

Refine your SMART aim Claude

Give Claude your rough idea. Ask it to refine it into a SMART aim meeting RCEM SLO 11 requirements — measurable outcome, baseline, target, population, timeframe.

2

Root cause analysis ChatGPT

Ask for a fishbone diagram using the 6M framework in Mermaid syntax. Paste the code into mermaid.live to render and export it as an image — free, no account needed.

3

Driver diagram ChatGPT

Ask for a driver diagram: Aim → Primary Drivers → Secondary Drivers → Change Ideas. Render and export from mermaid.live.

4

Baseline SPC analysis ChatGPT

Upload your CSV. Generate a run chart or SPC chart showing baseline performance, median, and variation.

5

PDSA planning Claude

Ask Claude to structure your interventions as sequential PDSA cycles, starting small. It will help you think through what to measure at each stage.

6

Post-intervention analysis and portfolio report ChatGPT Claude

Upload post-intervention data for comparative SPC charts in ChatGPT. Ask Claude to structure the full report to RCEM QIAT (Quality Improvement Assessment Tool) format.

RCEM QIP themes 2025–26

  • →Mental Health — Self-Harm pathways
  • →Care of Older People and frailty
  • →Time Critical Medications
  • →All require PDSA methodology and SPC analysis
  • →New QIAT form (2025) — Claude can scaffold the structured reflection

Key prompting tip

Tell ChatGPT exactly which columns your data has, what each column means, and precisely what you want calculated. Vague prompts produce vague results.

Teaching & Education

Better teaching sessions in less preparation time

Whether you're running a registrar teaching session, preparing SHO induction content, or studying for your FRCEM, AI turns hours of preparation into minutes.

Designing a session

Give Claude the topic, audience level, time available, and learning objectives — and ask it to produce a full session plan with timings, teaching methods, key content, and case scenarios. The structure is done; you refine it.

Try this Claude
I'm running a 45-minute teaching session for ST1–ST3 EM trainees on assessing and managing AKI in the ED. Design a session plan with: 3–4 SMART learning objectives, a 5-minute opening case scenario, key content with timings, two MCQs for a mid-session check, and a concise summary. Make it interactive rather than didactic.

Generating SBA questions

AI-generated MCQs achieve around 93% factual accuracy (BMC Medical Education, 2025) — excellent for practice, but requiring clinical review before any formal assessment use.

Try this ChatGPT
Generate 5 FRCEM Intermediate SBA questions on acute pulmonary oedema. Each should include: a 3–4 sentence clinical vignette, five answer options (A–E), the correct answer with explanation, and the relevant NICE guideline or RCEM curriculum area.

NotebookLM for revision

NotebookLM is uniquely powerful for self-directed revision. Upload the guidelines you need to know and it becomes an expert on exactly those documents — and only those documents. It cannot hallucinate references from sources it hasn't been given. The Audio Overview feature generates a conversational podcast discussion you can listen to on your commute. You can now also customise the format (brief overview, deep dive, debate, or critique) and interrupt the AI hosts mid-episode to ask clarifying questions.

Socratic tutoring for FRCEM

Try this Claude
Act as an FRCEM examiner. Ask me viva-style questions on toxicology, starting basic and progressing to advanced over 10 questions. After each answer I give, score me out of 10, explain what I missed, then ask the next question. Don't reveal all questions upfront.

Teaching toolkit

  • →Claude — session design, case scenarios, SBA questions, handouts
  • →NotebookLM — turn any guideline into audio revision or a quiz
  • →ChatGPT — MCQ generation, Anki CSV export
  • →Gemini — build a presentation directly in Google Slides
  • →Copilot — build slides in PowerPoint from bullet notes

The Anki workflow

  • 1Ask ChatGPT to generate flashcards in CSV format (Question, Answer columns)
  • 2Import the CSV directly into Anki
  • 3Use spaced repetition for long-term retention
  • 4Anki AI Add-on (#643253121) integrates ChatGPT directly within Anki

Organisation & Productivity

The admin that doesn't have to take so long

Emails, meeting summaries, slide decks, literature updates — AI can draft all of it in seconds. The time savings are real and compound quickly.

Emails and professional communication

Describe the email you need to write, the recipient, the tone, and the key points. Gemini works directly inside Gmail. Copilot works inside Outlook. Claude is particularly good at getting tone right for sensitive or complex messages.

Gemini and Copilot inside your existing apps

If you use Google Workspace, Gemini is integrated directly into Docs, Sheets, Slides, Gmail, and Drive. If you use Microsoft, Copilot does the same across Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Outlook, and Teams. These integrations mean you can work with AI in context — the tool knows what document or email you have open — without any copy-pasting.

Meeting notes turned into action plans

After any governance meeting, MDT, or supervision session, paste your rough notes into Claude and ask for a structured summary with a numbered action list, owner, and suggested deadline for each item. Thirty seconds instead of fifteen minutes.

Staying current

Use Perplexity weekly for a literature update on your key topic areas. Ask it what has been published in the last month on a specific topic. Unlike a PubMed alert — which gives you a list of titles — Perplexity synthesises the findings and tells you what is clinically relevant.

Highest-return tasks to try first

  • 1Draft all routine emails with Gemini (Gmail) or Copilot (Outlook)
  • 2Paste meeting notes into Claude for a structured action plan
  • 3Use Perplexity weekly to stay current on key topics
  • 4Build teaching slide decks with Copilot in PowerPoint or Gemini in Slides
  • 5Ask Claude to summarise any long document or policy you receive

Privacy note

For non-patient administrative work, consumer AI tools are generally appropriate. Always check your Trust's IG policy. For anything involving patient data, HR matters, or confidential governance, do not use consumer AI tools.

Tool Guides

A closer look at each tool

Full capabilities and a quick-start guide for each of the six tools. Accurate as of early 2026.

🔍
Perplexity

An AI-powered answer engine that searches the web in real time and cites every claim. Described as the tool that makes "the blue link feel like a relic."

Free tier available Pro $20/mo perplexity.ai

Perplexity combines a real-time web search index with large language models to synthesise and cite answers. Unlike general-purpose chatbots, every response links directly to its sources — making it far more trustworthy for research. As of early 2026 it processes around 780 million searches per month and has moved to a subscription-first model (no advertising). Pro users can choose which underlying model to use — currently including GPT-5.1, Claude 4.6, and Gemini models.

Real-time web search with inline citations

Every answer cites its sources. Click any citation number to go directly to the original article, paper, or website. This is the fundamental feature that makes Perplexity trustworthy for evidence searching.

Deep Research mode

Conducts multi-step autonomous research: formulates a plan, runs dozens of parallel searches, cross-references findings, and produces a comprehensive cited report in under 4 minutes. Scores 93.9% on factual accuracy benchmarks. Available on Pro.

Academic Focus mode

Pro feature that restricts results to peer-reviewed academic sources — particularly useful for medical literature searching. Surfaces papers from journals rather than news or blog content.

Spaces for organised research

Create dedicated research spaces for ongoing projects — each space maintains context across sessions, so you can build a sustained literature search over time rather than starting from scratch each visit.

Internal document search (Pro/Enterprise)

Pro users can upload and search across their own files (Excel, Word, PDF) alongside web content simultaneously. Useful for cross-referencing your local audit data or Trust protocols against published evidence.

Model Council

New in 2026: compare outputs from multiple AI models simultaneously for the same query — currently including GPT-5.2 and Claude 4.6. Useful when you want multiple perspectives on a complex question.

Perplexity Pages

Generates structured, report-style summaries from user queries — useful for producing a shareable literature overview or background document on a QIP topic.

Quick-start guide

1
Go to perplexity.ai — no account needed for basic use. Create a free account to save searches.
2
Type a question in natural language. You don't need keywords — write like you're asking a colleague.
3
Click the numbered citations in the response to verify every claim in the original source.
4
For medical literature, select "Academic" from the Focus options before searching.
5
For a complex topic, select "Deep Research" mode — it takes 2–4 minutes but produces a thorough cited report.
6
Use Spaces to organise ongoing research projects (e.g. a QIP topic, a fellowship application).

Claude

Anthropic's AI assistant. Widely regarded as the most capable model for writing, reasoning, and reading long documents. The current version on claude.ai is Claude Sonnet 4.6.

Free tier Pro $20/mo claude.ai

Claude is built by Anthropic with a particular focus on safety, honesty, and nuanced reasoning. It tends to be more careful about acknowledging uncertainty than other models. Its 200,000-token context window (Pro) means it can process an entire NICE guideline, systematic review, or portfolio summary in a single conversation — a significant practical advantage.

200,000-token context window

Can process approximately 150,000 words in a single conversation — enough to hold an entire NICE guideline, multiple research papers, or a complete portfolio summary simultaneously. This is one of the largest context windows of any consumer AI tool.

Writing and editing

Consistently rated highly for nuanced, natural-sounding writing. Particularly good at matching your existing voice when asked, structuring reflections, drafting professional communications, and producing academic prose. Tends to give more honest, calibrated responses than competitors.

Document upload and analysis

Upload PDFs, Word documents, and text files for analysis. Particularly useful for guideline comparison, critical appraisal, summarising long policy documents, and cross-referencing multiple sources in one conversation.

Artifacts — interactive tools without coding

Ask Claude to build an interactive tool — a Wells Score calculator, an audit dashboard, or a teaching quiz — and it generates a working web application rendered live in the browser. Shareable via link. No coding knowledge required.

Web search (Pro)

Pro users can enable web search, allowing Claude to retrieve current information to supplement its responses. Useful when you need both the analytical depth of Claude and access to current sources.

Memory across conversations

Claude can retain information about you and your preferences across conversations — your role, interests, ongoing projects — so you don't have to re-explain context every session.

Projects

Organise related conversations into Projects with shared context and uploaded documents — useful for keeping a QIP project, fellowship application, or research topic in one coherent workspace.

Quick-start guide

1
Go to claude.ai and create a free account. The free tier is genuinely useful — Pro is worth it if you use it daily.
2
Use the paperclip icon to upload documents. Paste text directly into the chat for shorter content.
3
Be specific. Tell Claude your role, the purpose of the output, the audience, and the format you want.
4
Use Projects (Pro) to create a persistent workspace for ongoing work — attach documents and system instructions once.
5
Ask follow-up questions and iterate. Claude maintains the full conversation context, so you can refine outputs progressively.
6
For interactive tools (calculators, dashboards), ask "Build me an X" — the Artifact feature renders it live in the chat.

NotebookLM

Google's source-grounded research assistant. Upload your documents and it becomes an expert on exactly those documents — and cannot hallucinate references it hasn't been given. Has evolved significantly through 2025 and early 2026.

Free (100 notebooks, 50 queries/day) AI Pro $20/mo notebooklm.google.com

NotebookLM uses Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) — it only provides answers grounded in the specific documents you have uploaded. This is its most important property: it cannot confabulate references from sources it hasn't seen. Supports up to 1 million tokens per source (approximately 500,000 words). Accepts PDFs, Google Docs, websites, YouTube videos, and audio files. Has been substantially updated through 2025–2026 and now offers far more than just the original audio feature.

Audio Overviews (podcasts from your documents)

The original breakout feature. Uploads any document and generates a 10–20 minute conversational podcast between two AI hosts who explain, contextualise, and discuss your content. Now customisable: choose between a brief overview, a deep dive, a debate format, or a critical review. You can also specify a particular focus area (e.g. "focus on the ED-specific recommendations only").

Interactive Audio — interrupt the podcast

New in 2025–2026: while an Audio Overview is playing, you can tap "Join" to interrupt the AI hosts and ask a clarifying question. They stop, answer using your source documents, and then continue. Turns a passive listening experience into an active tutorial session.

Quizzes and flashcards

The Studio panel generates instant quizzes and flashcards directly from your uploaded sources. Useful for generating practice questions from NICE guidelines or RCEM best practice documents — grounded in your specific materials rather than general knowledge.

Mind maps

New interactive feature that visualises the connections between concepts in your uploaded documents. Helps navigate complex topics and understand relationships between ideas — useful for revision or when trying to understand a new guideline's structure.

Briefing documents, study guides, FAQs

The Studio panel generates structured outputs from your sources: briefing documents (executive summaries), study guides (comprehensive notes with definitions), FAQ documents, and timeline summaries. One-click generation from any source.

Slide decks and infographics

New in 2025–2026: the Studio panel can now generate presentation slide decks and infographics from your uploaded sources — useful for rapidly creating teaching materials from a guideline you've uploaded.

Data tables

New feature: upload documents and ask NotebookLM to extract structured data into a comparison table. Useful for comparing multiple guidelines or extracting key data points from multiple papers simultaneously.

Deep Research (paid)

Introduced in November 2025. Goes beyond answering from your uploaded notes — actively searches for new information to supplement your sources. Marks a shift from a purely passive tool to an active research agent.

Grounded Q&A chat

Ask any question and NotebookLM answers from your specific uploaded sources, citing the exact passage it drew from. All citations link to the relevant section of the source document.

Generate flashcards and quizzes from the Studio panel — useful for self-testing before an ARCP or exam.

While the audio is playing, tap "Join" to interrupt and ask a question — the hosts answer from your sources and continue.

Data governance advantage

Data entered into Microsoft 365 Copilot is processed within your organisation's Microsoft 365 trust boundary and does not train Microsoft's AI models. This makes it the most appropriate AI tool for drafting documents that reference non-identifiable organisational data (audit aggregate results, governance documents, policy drafts).

Project Manager Agent (rolling out 2026)

New feature in preview: an AI-powered agent that helps plan, organise, and manage work — tracking tasks, coordinating workflows, and providing updates. Potentially very useful for QIP project management within your Trust's M365 environment.

Quick-start guide

1
Check with your Trust IT team what Copilot features are enabled. Copilot Chat may already be available in your M365 apps.
2
In Word, Excel, or PowerPoint, look for the Copilot button in the toolbar (sparkle icon). This opens the Copilot Chat side pane.
3
In Outlook, the Copilot pane can summarise any email thread — open a thread and click the Copilot icon.
4
In Teams (full Copilot licence), enable meeting transcription before a meeting to get an automatic summary and action items afterwards.
5
For presentations, start in PowerPoint and type "Create a presentation about [topic]" in the Copilot pane — review all statistics it generates.
6
Never enter patient-identifiable data even in M365 Copilot — use it for aggregate data, governance documents, and professional writing only.

Where to start

One subscription. One week. Five different professional tasks. That's the honest getting-started guide.

Step one
Check what you already have
Ask your Trust IT team what Copilot features are enabled in your M365 account. You may already have AI capabilities in Outlook, Word, and Teams at no extra cost.
Step two
Add one paid subscription
Either ChatGPT Plus or Claude Pro, both around £16/month. This covers writing, data analysis, QIP work, and teaching content. You don't need both to start.
Step three
Set up the free tools
NotebookLM (free), Perplexity free tier, and Gemini free tier cost nothing. Set them up this week. NotebookLM alone is worth an hour of your time to explore — upload a guideline and generate an Audio Overview.
Step four
Use it every day for a week
Draft an email. Run a rough reflection through Claude. Upload a guideline to NotebookLM. Analyse a small dataset in ChatGPT. One week of daily use builds genuine intuition for what works.
