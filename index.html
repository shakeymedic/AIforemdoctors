01
Portfolio & Reflective Practice

Stop staring at a blank reflective log

AI won't write your portfolio for you — but it provides the scaffolding to write it yourself, faster and with better structure. RCEM's September 2025 position statement explicitly approves this approach.

Turning rough notes into a structured reflection

The hardest part of a reflective log is starting. Most trainees know what they want to say but can't shape it. The workflow is straightforward: jot bullet-point notes immediately after a significant case or experience — even a few sentences on your phone. Then use Claude to transform those raw notes into a Gibbs or Driscoll reflection. Your voice, your experience, your insight — just properly structured.

Try this Claude
Here are my rough notes from a resuscitation I led last week: [paste your bullet points]

Structure these into a reflective log using Gibbs' Reflective Cycle. Keep my voice and do not add any clinical details not in my notes. Where my reflection is thin, suggest specific questions I could consider to deepen it — don't answer them for me.

What RCEM says is and isn't acceptable

RCEM's September 2025 position statement is clear. Acceptable: using AI to structure reflections, improve clarity, suggest reflective prompts, and explore different perspectives. Not acceptable: fabricating experiences, submitting AI content without genuine personal input, or using AI to avoid reflecting at all. The experience and critical thinking must be genuinely yours.

ARCP gap analysis

Before an ARCP, paste a summary of your portfolio entries into Claude and ask it to map them against the RCEM curriculum. Ask it to identify thin areas and suggest what types of entries would fill gaps. This turns a daunting exercise into a clear action plan.

Try this Claude
Here is a summary of my portfolio entries from the past year: [paste summary]

I'm an ST4 EM trainee. Map my evidence against the RCEM curriculum competencies. Tell me which areas look well-evidenced, which look thin, and suggest what types of portfolio entries would fill the gaps before my ARCP.

Personal statements and applications

Give Claude your raw notes about your achievements and what the post requires — then ask it to identify your strongest narrative thread before drafting anything. This is more useful than asking it to write a statement from scratch, as it forces clarity about what makes your application distinctive.

Which tool to use

  • →Claude — best for writing and long documents. Its large context window can hold your entire portfolio summary.
  • →Copilot — if your portfolio is in Word, Copilot can work directly within the document.
  • →Gemini — ideal if your portfolio is in Google Docs; works in-context without copy-pasting.

The RCEM 2025 position — key phrase

"AI may be used to structure reflections and enhance clarity, but the experience, insight, and critical thinking must be genuinely the trainee's own."

Try these this week

1
The three-minute reflection. Open your phone's notes app after your next shift. Write four or five bullet points about a case or interaction that stuck with you — rough, unpolished, no structure. Paste them into Claude with the Gibbs prompt from this section. See what comes back.
2
ARCP gap check. Write a rough summary of your last three months' portfolio entries (a few sentences per entry is enough). Ask Claude to map them against the RCEM curriculum and identify where you are thin. If the output surprises you, that is useful information before your ARCP, not after.
3
Tone test. Think of a professional email you have been putting off writing — a difficult request, an awkward follow-up, a disagreement. Ask Claude to draft it. Notice how it handles the tone. Edit it into your own voice. Compare how long that took versus writing it from scratch.

Research & Evidence

Find the evidence. Understand the paper. In half the time.

AI has transformed literature searching. Know which tool to use — and remember that all of them can still misrepresent sources. Always verify cited references yourself before using them.

Start with Perplexity for any literature question

Perplexity is the first port of call for any evidence question. It searches the web in real time and cites every claim, so you can click through to the source paper directly. Think of it as a research assistant who has already read everything and synthesises it with references attached. Its Deep Research mode conducts multi-step autonomous research, producing comprehensive reports in under four minutes.

Try this Perplexity
What does the current evidence say about point-of-care ultrasound for diagnosing pulmonary oedema in the emergency department? Summarise the key RCTs and systematic reviews from the last five years, including sample sizes, key findings, and limitations. Focus on UK-applicable evidence.

Go deeper with Claude for full document analysis

Once Perplexity has given you an overview, use Claude for the detailed work. You can upload entire systematic reviews, NICE guidelines, and RCEM best practice documents and interrogate them all in a single conversation — particularly useful for guideline comparison and critical appraisal preparation.

Try this Claude
I've uploaded this systematic review. Help me critically appraise it using the CASP framework. Identify the main strengths and weaknesses, assess the risk of bias across included studies, and tell me how applicable the findings are to a UK district general ED. Be direct about where the evidence is weak.

Journal club prep: the three-tool workflow

Upload the paper to NotebookLM and generate an Audio Overview to understand the broad picture on your commute. Upload it to Claude for a structured CASP appraisal. Then use Perplexity to find papers that support or challenge the findings. What used to take a Sunday afternoon now takes about 20 minutes.

Verify every reference

ChatGPT and Claude can fabricate paper references with plausible-sounding authors, journals, and DOIs. This is known as hallucination. Always verify any cited reference yourself in PubMed or Google Scholar before including it in anything you write or present. Perplexity is safer because it links to real sources, but it can still misrepresent what those sources actually say.

  • 2Claude — upload full papers and guidelines for in-depth appraisal and comparison.
  • 3NotebookLM — convert papers into audio for commute listening.

QIP & Data Analysis

Your audit data analysed — without a statistician

ChatGPT's Code Interpreter is genuinely transformative for doctors with audit data and no coding background. Upload a spreadsheet, describe what you want, and it writes and runs the analysis for you.

Analysing your data

ChatGPT Plus includes Code Interpreter (Advanced Data Analysis). Upload a CSV or Excel file, describe what you want in plain English, and it writes the Python code, runs it, and returns charts and results. No coding required.

Try this ChatGPT
I've uploaded a CSV of door-to-antibiotic times for sepsis patients over three months. Columns: date, time_minutes, shift (day/evening/night), day_of_week.

1. Calculate median and IQR for door-to-antibiotic time
2. Show what percentage met the 60-minute target
3. Break down by shift and day of week to identify patterns
4. Create a histogram of the distribution

SPC charts for RCEM QIPs

SPC charts are required for RCEM portfolio QIP submissions. ChatGPT generates them from your data — including mean, UCL, LCL, and automatic flagging of rule violations.

Try this ChatGPT
Using the uploaded data, create an I-MR SPC chart. Calculate mean, UCL, and LCL. Highlight any points violating standard SPC rules: a point beyond 3 sigma, 8+ consecutive points one side of the mean, or 6 consecutive points in a trend. Label each violation clearly on the chart and explain what it means.

Teaching & Education

Better teaching sessions in less preparation time

Whether you're running a registrar teaching session, preparing SHO induction content, or studying for your FRCEM, AI turns hours of preparation into minutes.

Designing a session

Give Claude the topic, audience level, time available, and learning objectives — and ask it to produce a full session plan with timings, teaching methods, key content, and case scenarios. The structure is done; you refine it.

Try this Claude
I'm running a 45-minute teaching session for ST1–ST3 EM trainees on assessing and managing AKI in the ED. Design a session plan with: 3–4 SMART learning objectives, a 5-minute opening case scenario, key content with timings, two MCQs for a mid-session check, and a concise summary. Make it interactive rather than didactic.

Generating SBA questions

AI-generated MCQs achieve around 93% factual accuracy (BMC Medical Education, 2025) — excellent for practice, but requiring clinical review before any formal assessment use.

Try this ChatGPT
Generate 5 FRCEM Intermediate SBA questions on acute pulmonary oedema. Each should include: a 3–4 sentence clinical vignette, five answer options (A–E), the correct answer with explanation, and the relevant NICE guideline or RCEM curriculum area.

NotebookLM for revision

NotebookLM is uniquely powerful for self-directed revision. Upload the guidelines you need to know and it becomes an expert on exactly those documents — and only those documents. It cannot hallucinate references from sources it hasn't been given. The Audio Overview feature generates a conversational podcast discussion you can listen to on your commute. You can now also customise the format (brief overview, deep dive, debate, or critique) and interrupt the AI hosts mid-episode to ask clarifying questions.

Socratic tutoring for FRCEM

Try this Claude
Act as an FRCEM examiner. Ask me viva-style questions on toxicology, starting basic and progressing to advanced over 10 questions. After each answer I give, score me out of 10, explain what I missed, then ask the next question. Don't reveal all questions upfront.

Prompting Principles

Why good prompting matters — and how to do it

Knowing a tool exists and being fluent with it are different things. This section explains the four underlying principles that make the difference between prompts that produce impressive outputs and prompts that produce mediocre ones. Understanding why these principles work — not just that they work — lets you apply them to any AI tool, any task, in any situation.

Why prompting skill matters

All of these AI tools are, at their core, next-word prediction systems that have been trained to be helpful. They have no inherent understanding of your situation, your role, or what you actually need. Everything they know about your specific task comes from what you tell them in the prompt. The more precisely and completely you communicate your context, purpose, and constraints, the better the output will be.

The most common reason a doctor abandons an AI tool after one or two attempts is that they used a vague prompt, got a generic response, concluded "it doesn't work," and stopped. The tool wasn't the problem. What follows are the four principles that prevent this.

1 Role and context first

Tell the model who you are and what the output is for before you ask it to do anything. This single change produces the largest improvement in output quality. "I am an ST5 emergency medicine registrar in the UK preparing for an ARCP" fundamentally changes every response compared to asking the same question with no context. The model uses your stated role to calibrate depth, terminology, assumed knowledge, and relevance.

2 Specify the format explicitly

Tell the model exactly what you want it to produce — not just the topic, but the structure, length, audience, and purpose. "Write a Gibbs reflection" is better than "help me reflect." "Produce a 250-word structured abstract suitable for an RCEM portfolio submission" is better than "write a summary." The model cannot guess your intended format and will default to something generic unless you specify.

3 Constrain what it should not do

Negative constraints are underused and highly effective. Telling the model what to avoid is often as important as telling it what to produce. "Do not add clinical details not in my notes" prevents hallucination in reflective writing. "Do not use bullet points — write in prose" controls format. "Do not speculate beyond the uploaded document" keeps analysis grounded. Constraints narrow the model's degrees of freedom toward the output you actually want.

4 Iterate, do not restart

The most common mistake after receiving an unsatisfactory response is abandoning the conversation and starting again. This throws away the accumulated context. Instead, follow up within the same conversation: "This is close but the tone is too formal — rewrite it to sound more like spoken English." "The second paragraph is too long — condense it to three sentences." "You missed the point about X — revise the conclusion to address this." Each iteration costs seconds and moves toward a far better output than any single prompt will produce.

Seeing the difference: the same request, two ways

Here is the same underlying request — help drafting a reflective log — written first as a typical first attempt, and then applying all four principles.

❌ Typical first attempt
Can you help me write a reflection about a difficult case?
✓ Applying all four principles
I am an ST3 emergency medicine registrar in the UK. I need to write a portfolio reflective log using Gibbs' Reflective Cycle for my ARCP. Here are my rough notes from a difficult resuscitation last week: [notes]. Structure these into a Gibbs reflection. Keep my voice and do not add any clinical details that are not in my notes. Where my reflection is thin, ask me questions to help me develop it — do not fill in the gaps yourself. Keep the total length to around 400 words.
❌ Vague data request
Can you analyse my audit data?
✓ Specific and constrained
I have uploaded a CSV. It has three columns: date (DD/MM/YYYY format), time_to_antibiotic_minutes (integer), and shift (one of: day, evening, night). Calculate the median and IQR for the whole dataset. Then break down the median by shift type and by day of week. Show the results as a table and as a bar chart. Do not produce any other statistics unless I ask.

One more principle: give it room to push back

These tools are calibrated to be helpful, which sometimes means they agree with you too readily or produce what you seem to want rather than what you actually need. Explicitly inviting critical feedback changes this. Adding "identify the weaknesses in this argument" or "tell me what I've missed" or "what would a sceptical reviewer object to?" gives the model permission to be genuinely useful rather than merely agreeable. This is particularly valuable for drafts of QIP reports, portfolio submissions, and research writing.

The four principles at a glance

  • 1Role and context first — who you are and what the output is for
  • 2Specify the format — structure, length, audience, purpose
  • 3Constrain what to avoid — negative constraints prevent the most common failures
  • 4Iterate, don't restart — follow up within the same conversation

Quick prompt checklist

  • →Have you told it your role and specialty?
  • →Have you specified the exact format you want?
  • →Have you told it what not to do?
  • →Have you given it a length target?
  • →Have you named the audience for the output?
  • →If unsatisfied — have you iterated rather than restarted?

The broader principle

These four principles work because they reduce the gap between what you have in your head and what the model has to work with. The model can only produce what you communicate. Prompting fluency is the skill of closing that gap.

C
Critical Appraisal of AI Output

How to think critically about what AI tells you

Using AI tools well is not just about getting good outputs — it's about knowing when to trust those outputs and when to be sceptical. This is a learnable cognitive skill, analogous to critical appraisal of the medical literature. These tools fail in predictable ways, and understanding how they fail makes you a significantly more effective and safer user.

The three failure modes to know

Every AI output failure that matters in professional use falls into one of three categories. Each has a distinct character and a different mitigation strategy.

Highest risk
Hallucination — confident fabrication

The model generates factually incorrect information with full confidence. Most commonly: fabricated references (plausible authors, journals, and DOIs that do not exist), invented statistics, and non-existent guidelines. The output reads as authoritative and is wrong. Mitigation: verify every factual claim against a primary source before relying on it.

Medium risk
Sycophancy — agreeing too readily

The model agrees with your premise even when your premise is wrong, or produces the answer it thinks you want rather than the accurate one. If you present a flawed argument and ask it to improve it, it may improve the writing without flagging the flaw. Mitigation: explicitly ask it to critique, challenge, or find weaknesses — don't just ask it to agree or improve.

Lower but real risk
Confidently outdated — training cutoffs

Models are trained on data up to a cutoff date and have no knowledge of more recent events, guideline updates, or new evidence. They will answer questions about current guidelines with apparent authority even when those guidelines have changed. Mitigation: use Perplexity (which searches in real time) for anything requiring current information.

Recognising hallucination in practice

Hallucinated references are the most common and most consequential failure for clinical professionals. They follow a recognisable pattern: the author names sound plausible, the journal is credible, the year is recent, the title is specific and relevant. The paper simply does not exist. Hallucinated statistics have a similar character — precise, plausible, and wrong.

The practical test is simple: before including any AI-provided reference, search for it in PubMed using the exact title and lead author. If it is not there, it is not real. Do not search by topic and conclude "well, something similar probably exists." Search for the specific paper. There is no shortcut to this step.

Prompts that actively reduce hallucination risk
"If you are not certain about a fact, say so explicitly rather than guessing."

"Do not cite specific papers or statistics unless you are confident they exist. If you are unsure, say 'I believe there is evidence for this but you should verify' rather than providing a reference."

"Only answer from the document I have uploaded. If the document does not address this question, say so."

The accountability question

The GMC's 2024 guidance is clear on a point that is easy to overlook when a polished AI output is in front of you: you remain professionally responsible for every piece of work you submit, present, or act upon, regardless of whether it was assisted by AI. An AI-drafted reflective log that misrepresents your experience is a misrepresentation. An AI-generated slide that contains a fabricated statistic, which you present to colleagues, is your error. An AI-produced drug calculation that contains a mathematical mistake is your mistake if you did not check it.

This is not an argument against using AI. It is an argument for using it with the same critical appraisal you apply to any source — which is to say, actively and sceptically, not passively.

Towards AI literacy as a professional skill

The medical education literature is beginning to recognise that AI literacy — the ability to evaluate, adopt, and critically appraise AI tools — is a professional skill that belongs in the curriculum, not an optional technical interest. The RCEM curriculum currently contains no explicit AI literacy component. Neither does the GMC's Good Medical Practice framework, though its 2024 guidance gestures towards this.

The doctors who will use these tools most effectively are not those who adopt everything uncritically, nor those who reject everything sceptically, but those who bring the same rigorous, evidence-based thinking to AI outputs that they apply to clinical decisions and published research. That disposition — active, critical, calibrated — is what this guide is ultimately trying to build.

The five-question check

  • 1Is this factual claim verifiable? Have I checked it against a primary source?
  • 2Is this reference real? Have I searched PubMed for the exact title?
  • 3Does this reflect my actual experience? (For portfolio work — have I added anything I didn't intend?)
  • 4Is this current? Could a guideline or evidence base have changed since the model's training?
  • 5Would I stand behind this? If my name is on it, am I confident in its accuracy?

Professional responsibility — the key principle

  • →You are responsible for everything you submit, present, or act upon
  • →"The AI wrote it" is not a defence under GMC or RCEM standards
  • →AI assistance requires the same critical appraisal as any other source
  • →RCEM Sep 2025: insight and critical thinking must be genuinely the trainee's own

The useful mental model

Treat every AI output the way you would treat information from a very confident, very fast, occasionally unreliable junior colleague. You act on what they tell you — but you verify before you commit, and the responsibility for the decision remains yours.

Try these this week

1
The hallucination test (do this once). Ask any general-purpose AI tool — ChatGPT or Claude — to give you five references on a specific clinical topic in EM. Search every single one in PubMed using the exact title. Note what exists, what is plausible but wrong, and what is entirely invented. This exercise produces a calibrated, lasting scepticism that no description can substitute for.
2
Sycophancy test. Write a brief argument about a clinical topic that contains a deliberate error — a wrong threshold, a misattributed guideline. Ask Claude to "review and improve this." See whether it corrects the error or polishes the prose around it. Then add "identify any factual errors in this" and compare the responses. This demonstrates sycophancy concretely and shows you how to mitigate it.
3
Apply the five-question check. Take the last piece of AI-assisted output you produced — an email, a reflection draft, a data chart. Run each of the five questions in the sidebar against it. This takes two minutes. If you find something you should have caught, that discomfort is productive — it calibrates your checking habit for next time.

Agentic Browsers

What if your browser could just do things for you?

Agentic browsers are the newest development in AI tooling. Rather than answering questions, they actively operate the web on your behalf — navigating pages, filling forms, drafting emails, and completing multi-step tasks while you focus on something else. This is early technology, and it comes with real limitations and risks that are worth understanding before you try it.

What "agentic" actually means

Every AI tool discussed in this guide so far is a conversational assistant — you ask, it responds. An agentic tool goes further: it takes actions. It can open a web page, read what is on it, click a link, fill in a form, open your email, draft a reply, and send it — all in sequence, in response to a single instruction from you. Think of it less like a calculator and more like having a capable assistant who can take over your keyboard.

This capability is genuinely new. The first usable consumer agentic browser — Perplexity's Comet — launched in July 2025. Others are following. We are at a very early stage, and the technology is not yet reliable enough for any task where an error would have serious consequences.

Comet — the Perplexity AI browser

Comet is a web browser built by Perplexity, based on Chromium (the same engine underlying Google Chrome). This means it looks and feels like a familiar browser, supports your existing Chrome extensions, and imports your bookmarks, history, and passwords from Chrome in a few clicks. What distinguishes it is the AI assistant built into its core — powered by Perplexity's search engine and, for Max subscribers, the Claude Opus 4.6 model.

It launched on desktop in July 2025, became free to all Perplexity account holders in October 2025, launched on Android in November 2025, and arrived on iOS on 18 March 2026. As of now, the basic browser and its agent mode are free — you only need a Perplexity account.

🔍 Highlighted text — instant answers

Highlight any text on any webpage and get an instant Perplexity-powered explanation, definition, or related context — without leaving the page or opening a new tab. Particularly useful when reading a complex guideline or research paper.

📑 Cross-tab synthesis

Ask Comet to summarise and compare content across all your open tabs simultaneously. Useful when you have several papers, guidelines, or policy documents open and want a synthesis without reading each one in full.

🤖 Browser agent — takes actions on your behalf

The core agentic feature. Give Comet a task in natural language — "find the three most recent systematic reviews on point-of-care lactate measurement and summarise their conclusions" — and it will search, navigate, read, and compile a report for you. It shows every action it is taking, and you can intervene at any point.

📅 Calendar and email integration

Comet can connect to your calendar and email to carry out tasks contextually — "draft a reply to the last email from my educational supervisor" or "check if I have any clinical governance commitments next week." Requires connecting your accounts.

🎙️ Voice mode

On Android and desktop, you can speak to the Comet assistant directly. Ask questions or give instructions verbally. Useful for hands-free queries or when dictating rough notes you want turned into a structured document.

🧠 Personalised preferences memory

Comet remembers your stated preferences and uses them when completing tasks — if you tell it you prefer concise summaries in a particular format, it applies this automatically to subsequent tasks without you needing to repeat it.

Example tasks for Comet's agent mode Comet
"Search for any RCEM publications or position statements from the last six months and summarise what is new."

"I have five tabs open with papers on frailty scoring in the ED. Summarise the key findings of each and identify where they agree and disagree."

"Find the current NICE guidance on sepsis management and produce a one-page summary of the ED-relevant recommendations."
⚠ Security and safety considerations — please read before using

Agentic browsers are powerful precisely because they can take real actions on your behalf. This is also what makes them risky. A misunderstood instruction can trigger a chain of unintended actions — sending an email you didn't intend, submitting a form, or making a booking. Cybersecurity researchers have identified a specific vulnerability in Comet called CometJacking, in which malicious content on a webpage could potentially instruct the browser agent to exfiltrate sensitive data. Perplexity was notified of this in August 2025; verify their current response before relying on the browser for sensitive tasks.

For professional use: never connect your NHS or Trust email account to Comet or any agentic browser. Do not use agent mode on pages containing any patient or staff information. Do not give agent mode access to any systems containing confidential data. Use it only for tasks involving publicly available information — literature searching, summarising open-access guidelines, and research browsing.

Practical uses for EM doctors — with appropriate caution

Despite the caveats, there are genuinely useful, low-risk applications of agentic browsing for professional work. The key is to use it only for tasks involving publicly available information, to review every action before it is executed, and to never connect it to clinical or confidential systems.

✓

Literature trawling

Ask Comet to search for recent publications on a specific clinical topic, open the most relevant papers, and compile a structured summary of findings — saving you an hour of manual searching and tab management.

✓

Guideline monitoring

Ask Comet to check the NICE, RCEM, and BNF websites for updates on topics relevant to your practice or current QIP, and summarise anything new since a specified date.

✓

Cross-tab synthesis for journal club

Open four or five papers related to a journal club topic, then ask Comet to read all of them and produce a structured comparison of their populations, methods, results, and limitations.

✗

Do not use for: anything involving confidential data

Do not connect NHS email, clinical systems, or Trust SharePoint to any agentic browser. Do not use agent mode on pages containing patient information or identifiable staff data. The technology is not mature enough for this, and the data governance implications remain unresolved.

The honest verdict

Comet is genuinely impressive and occasionally shockingly useful for literature searching and multi-tab synthesis. It is also unfinished and still makes errors. The right mental model is: a capable but junior assistant you need to supervise, not a fully reliable autonomous agent. Review every action it proposes before letting it execute.

Where to start

One subscription. One week. Five different professional tasks. That's the honest getting-started guide.

Step one
Check what you already have
Ask your Trust IT team what Copilot features are enabled in your M365 account. You may already have AI in Outlook, Word, and Teams at no extra cost. Also download Comet (free) from perplexity.ai/comet — it takes five minutes and costs nothing.
Step two
Add one paid subscription
Either ChatGPT Plus or Claude Pro, both around £16/month. This covers writing, data analysis, QIP work, and teaching content. You don't need both to start.
Step three
Set up the free tools
NotebookLM (free), Perplexity free tier, and Gemini free tier cost nothing. Set them up this week. NotebookLM alone is worth an hour of your time to explore — upload a guideline and generate an Audio Overview.
Step four
Use it every day for a week
Draft an email. Run a rough reflection through Claude. Upload a guideline to NotebookLM. Analyse a small dataset in ChatGPT. One week of daily use builds genuine intuition for what works.
